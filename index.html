Kafka

Partitioning is a product decision

Partitioning determines what you can guarantee (ordering), what you can scale (parallel consumers), and where you’ll feel pain (hot keys, replays, migrations).

What I optimize for
  • Stable keys that align with business invariants (e.g., accountId, orderId).
  • Hot-key mitigation via bucketing, secondary routing, or load-shedding paths.
  • Replay-safe consumers with idempotency and deterministic side effects.
Trade-offs I communicate early
  • Ordering is per-partition, not global.
  • More partitions increases parallelism but raises operational overhead.
  • Key changes are migrations: plan for dual-write/dual-read or re-key backfills.
Cassandra

Model for reads; pay for mistakes later

Cassandra is excellent when your queries are known and stable. It’s unforgiving when you need ad-hoc filtering or when your partition strategy creates hotspots.

Rules of thumb that hold up
  • Design tables per access pattern (denormalize intentionally).
  • Bound partition size using time buckets or sharding keys.
  • Watch tombstones (TTL, deletes, and wide rows can silently degrade reads).
Operational considerations
  • Choose compaction and TTL strategy with real traffic profiles.
  • Plan repairs and schema evolution as part of delivery, not after.
  • Prefer predictable queries over “flexible” ones that break SLOs under peak.
Caching

Cache correctness is a spectrum

“Perfect invalidation” is rarely worth it. Mature systems choose a bounded staleness model with explicit failure modes and guardrails against stampedes.

Patterns I reach for
  • Request coalescing to prevent thundering herds on hot items.
  • Stale-while-revalidate for high-read endpoints with safe freshness windows.
  • Soft TTL + hard TTL to control staleness and bound worst-case misses.
Where teams get surprised
  • Cache outages can be worse than DB outages if clients don’t degrade gracefully.
  • Invalidation storms are write amplification—rate limit and batch where possible.
  • Measure hit rate by key cohort (not just global) to find pathological traffic.
Async pipelines

Async systems need “replay safety”

Once you introduce queues and retries, duplicate processing is normal. The goal is not preventing duplicates—it’s making duplicates safe and observable.

Baseline design
  • Idempotency keys at boundaries (API → topic, topic → DB, DB → downstream).
  • Deterministic handlers so replay produces the same side effects.
  • Backpressure signals (queue depth, lag, saturation) tied to SLOs.
Operational maturity
  • Dead-letter queues with a human workflow (reprocess, drop, or compensate).
  • Replay tooling that’s safe by default (rate-limited, scoped, and auditable).
  • Retries tuned by failure domain (timeouts vs. validation vs. downstream limits).
Reliability

SLOs prevent “alert-driven” engineering

Mature teams don’t chase every spike. They define user-visible success and allocate engineering time with error budgets.

What works in practice
  • Define SLIs users care about (success rate, latency, freshness).
  • Map alerts to actions (who responds, what changes, and what’s the playbook).
  • Use budgets to decide when to pause feature work and pay down reliability debt.
Avoid these traps
  • Alerting on symptoms without context (CPU high) instead of impact (requests failing).
  • Dashboards without ownership—every chart should answer a question.
  • “One size fits all” thresholds—different endpoints have different risk profiles.
Data consistency

Consistency is a workflow, not a toggle

Distributed systems drift. The safest approach is designing for detection and repair: invariants, audits, and reconciliation loops.

Practical toolkit
  • Invariants (e.g., totals, monotonic states) that can be verified continuously.
  • Audit logs that allow replay and explainability during incidents.
  • Reconciliation jobs that repair missed writes and correct drift safely.
Trade-offs I make explicit
  • When strong consistency is required, constrain scope and keep failure domains small.
  • For eventual consistency, define freshness windows and user expectations clearly.
  • Prefer safe, observable compensations over hidden “best effort” behaviors.
